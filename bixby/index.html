Common Signs of a Termite Infestation

Recognizing the subtle, and sometimes not so subtle, hints that termites are present is key to safeguarding your property. Here are some of the most common indicators that you may have a termite problem requiring professional intervention:

Mud Tubes

Subterranean termites, a common type found in Bixby, construct mud tubes (shelter tubes) that are about the width of a pencil. These tubes are typically found on exterior or interior walls, connecting the termites' underground colony to their food source – your home's wood structure. These tubes protect the termites from predators and dry environments as they travel. Finding mud tubes is a significant indicator of active subterranean termite presence and warrants immediate professional inspection and treatment. The presence of even a single mud tube should be taken seriously as it signifies that termites are actively foraging for wood within or around your property. These structures are carefully constructed by worker termites using soil and their own saliva, providing a safe highway for them to access your home's wooden elements without being exposed to open air. The location and size of mud tubes can provide valuable insights into the extent and activity level of the termite colony. Our technicians are highly skilled at identifying these tubes, even those that are not immediately obvious, ensuring a comprehensive assessment of your termite situation. Ignoring mud tubes can lead to significant structural damage over time as the termites continue to feed on the wood within your walls and foundation. Early detection of these tubes is often the first visible sign homeowners notice, prompting them to seek professional help before the infestation escalates further. We strongly advise against disturbing or destroying mud tubes without a professional assessment, as this can simply cause the termites to reroute and make detection more difficult for future inspections.

Discarded Wings

Termite swarms, which typically occur in warmer months, involve reproductive termites (swarmers) leaving the colony to establish new ones. After swarming, these termites shed their wings. Finding small, translucent wings, often in piles near windowsills, doorways, or light fixtures, is a strong indication of a recent termite swarm. While the swarmers themselves may die off, the presence of discarded wings means a mature termite colony is nearby and has successfully reproduced. This is a clear signal that an existing infestation is likely present and requires prompt professional attention. The appearance of discarded wings is a critical warning sign that a termite colony has reached maturity and is actively expanding its population. Swarming is the natural process by which termites spread to new locations, and while the swarm itself might be brief, the discarded wings serve as lasting evidence of their presence. These wings are often mistaken for ant wings by untrained eyes, but a closer look reveals their unique shape and size. Our experts are trained to distinguish termite wings from other insect wings, ensuring an accurate diagnosis of your pest problem. Finding wings indoors suggests that the termite colony is either already within your home or in very close proximity, using your property as a launching point for new colonies. The timing of swarming can vary depending on the termite species and local weather conditions, but it is a common event in the Bixby area. Do not ignore piles of small wings; they are a definitive sign that professional termite control is needed to prevent further infestation and damage to your property.

Hollow-Sounding Wood

Termites consume wood from the inside out, often leaving a thin veneer of wood or paint on the surface. If you tap on a suspected wooden area and it sounds hollow or papery, it could indicate that termites have been actively feeding on the interior. This is a sign of potentially significant hidden damage. As termites tunnel through and consume the cellulose in wood, they create hollowed-out galleries. Tapping on affected areas can reveal these internal voids. This test should be performed carefully as applying too much pressure could expose the termite activity. Experienced termite professionals know exactly where to tap and what sounds to listen for to identify areas of concern. This is a more advanced sign of an infestation, suggesting that termites have been active in that location for some time and have already caused internal damage to the wooden structure. While the exterior of the wood may appear intact, the internal integrity is compromised, weakening the structure. This sign often accompanies other indicators like sagging floors or difficulties with windows and doors. If you suspect any wood in your home or business sounds hollow, avoid applying excessive pressure or attempting DIY repairs before a professional assessment, as this could exacerbate the problem or make evaluation more challenging. A professional inspection using specialized tools can confirm the extent of the internal damage and guide the necessary treatment strategy.

Sagging Floors or Ceilings

As termites consume the structural wood within floors and ceilings, these areas can lose their integrity and begin to sag or buckle. This is a more serious and often more visible sign of significant termite damage. If you notice any unusual dips or unevenness in your floors or ceilings, especially in older properties or around moisture-prone areas like bathrooms or kitchens, investigate it immediately as a potential sign of a severe termite infestation. Sagging floors or ceilings are a clear indication that the load-bearing wooden elements within your property's structure have been compromised by termite activity. This level of damage usually suggests a long-standing and widespread infestation that has gone unnoticed for a significant period. Structural damage caused by termites is not only costly to repair but can also pose safety risks. If you observe sagging, buckling, or unusual unevenness in any part of your floors or ceilings, it is imperative to contact a qualified structural engineer and a termite control professional immediately to assess the extent of the damage and the active termite infestation. Addressing both the pest issue and the structural repairs is essential to restoring the safety and integrity of your property. Do not attempt to walk on severely sagging floors or support compromised ceilings, as this could be dangerous. Our termite control services work in conjunction with necessary structural repairs to ensure a safe and long-lasting solution to termite damage.

Difficulties Opening Windows or Doors

Termite activity can cause wood to warp and swell, especially around door and window frames. As termites tunnel through these frames, they can distort the wood, making it difficult to open or close windows and doors smoothly. If you notice windows or doors becoming increasingly stiff or difficult to operate without an obvious cause like humidity changes, it could be a sign of termite damage within the frames. This is a less discussed but equally important indicator of potential termite activity in your property. The subtle changes in how your windows and doors function could be the first clue to a hidden infestation. As termites consume the wood in the frames, the structural integrity is compromised, leading to misalignment and difficulty in operation. This issue can gradually worsen over time as the termites continue to feed and the damage accumulates. If you've tried adjusting or lubricating windows and doors without success and the problem persists, consider the possibility of termite infestation. This sign is particularly relevant for wooden window and door frames. While humidity can cause some swelling, persistent difficulty opening or closing is more indicative of a structural issue caused by pests like termites. A thorough inspection by a termite professional can determine if termite activity is the underlying cause of these functional problems with your windows and doors. Addressing the termite issue will protect the remaining structural integrity and allow for necessary repairs or replacements of damaged frames.

Musty Odors

Some termite species, particularly subterranean termites, create a distinct musty or moldy odor. This smell is often more noticeable in confined spaces like basements or crawl spaces where humidity levels are higher and ventilation is limited. This odor can sometimes be mistaken for mold or mildew, which also thrive in damp environments preferred by termites. If you detect an unexplained musty odor in your home, especially in areas with potential moisture problems, it's worth investigating as a possible sign of a hidden termite infestation. The musty odor associated with some termite species is a result of their waste products and the moisture they introduce or are attracted to. This smell is often more pronounced in areas with poor ventilation and high humidity, conditions that are also favorable for termite colonies. Homeowners might initially dismiss this odor as simply a sign of dampness or mold, but if it persists despite efforts to improve ventilation or address moisture sources, it could be indicative of active termite presence. The odor can also emanate from areas where termites are actively tunneling through and depositing fecal matter. If you notice a persistent musty smell that you cannot attribute to other sources, particularly in basements, crawl spaces, or near wooden structures, consider it a potential warning sign of termites. A professional termite inspection can help differentiate between a moisture issue, mold growth, and an active termite infestation, ensuring that the correct problem is identified and addressed effectively to protect your property and your health.

Frass (Termite Droppings)

Drywood termites, which are less common in Bixby than subterranean termites but can still be found, leave behind small, pellet-like droppings called frass. This frass is often found in small piles near infested wood or kick-out holes (tiny holes through which termites push their waste out of the wood). The appearance of frass is a clear indication of a drywood termite infestation. The color of the frass often matches the color of the wood the termites are consuming. Frass is essentially termite fecal pellets and is a tell-tale sign of drywood termite activity. Unlike subterranean termites that tunnel through soil, drywood termites live entirely within the wood they infest, creating a network of galleries and expelling their waste through small openings called 'kick-out' holes. These tiny, often hexagonal pellets accumulate in small piles directly below the infested wood, resembling sawdust or fine sand. The presence of even small piles of frass indicates that termites are actively feeding and tunneling within the wood. Identifying frass and distinguishing it from sawdust or other debris is crucial for accurate diagnosis. Our trained technicians are adept at recognizing termite frass and locating the kick-out holes from which it originates. If you discover small, unexplained piles of pellet-like material near wooden furniture, walls, or structural beams, it's a strong indication of a potential drywood termite infestation requiring professional inspection and treatment to prevent further damage to the affected wood.
